<h2>What is Internal Linking?</h2><p>Internal linking refers to the practice of connecting one page of a website to another page within the same domain using hyperlinks. These connections allow both users and search engines to navigate through a website’s structure more efficiently, facilitating content discovery and improving user experience. Internal links can take several forms, such as navigation menus, contextual links within the body of a page, and related articles sections. By weaving a thoughtful network of connections, websites can distribute traffic and authority across their content, helping to maintain an organized hierarchy. According to <a href="https://moz.com/learn/seo/internal-link" target="_blank">industry standards on internal links</a>, a well-structured linking approach enhances not only navigability but also the overall visibility of important pages within search results. As referenced in site architecture documentation, internal linking is foundational to establishing logical pathways for users and clarifying content relationships for search engine crawlers. This discipline is essential for optimizing site performance, supporting seamless exploration, and ensuring that critical resources are easily found by both humans and automated indexing systems. The significance of internal linking extends beyond SEO, influencing site usability, engagement metrics, and the ability to surface high-value content at strategic points in the user’s journey.</p><h2>Synonyms</h2><ul><li>On-site navigation</li><li>Site interlinking</li><li>Cross-page linking</li><li>Intrasite hyperlinks</li><li>In-domain referencing</li></ul><h2>Examples</h2><p>Internal linking is commonly implemented in a variety of ways across digital properties, shaping the way users interact with content.
